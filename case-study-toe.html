Dialectica · Expert network

Rebuilding expert onboarding at Dialectica to lower compliance risk across 25,000+ experts a month

Dialectica is an expert network. Its Terms of Engagement flow is where experts, after receiving a secure link, review and update their professional experience and set the days and hours they are free for client calls. The point of the flow is data accuracy: Dialectica needs each expert's background to be current before a consultation goes ahead.

At 25,000 to 35,000 experts a month, that is not an edge concern. It is the front door to the whole product. I led the redesign end to end and built a small design system from scratch on a new visual language to ship it.

Context

The flow verifies 25,000 to 35,000 experts a month before they take client calls

After receiving a secure link, experts review and update their professional experience (roles, companies, responsibilities) and set the days and hours they are free for client calls. The flow exists for one reason: data accuracy. Dialectica needs each expert's background to be current before a consultation goes ahead.

At this volume it is the front door to the whole product. A later step, Profile Boost, also captures structured product-category and vendor data from experts; a v2 redesign of it appears as a design decision below.

Terms of engagement, the Compliance Tutorial and Profile Boost each reached the expert as a separate email, handled in isolation.

Problem area

The data Dialectica most needed verified was the easiest to scroll past

This was a business decision more than a user-pain fix. Two things have to be true before a client call: the expert has to be an accurate match for the project, and they have to be compliant and eligible to take it. The old flow made both harder to guarantee. Compliance sat in scattered pieces, and current experience had no more prominence than anything else on a crowded single page.

Status quo

Every expert passes through the flow before a client call. The Compliance Tutorial and Questionnaire arrived as separate emails, and current experience blended into a crowded page, so experts could move through without really reviewing it.

If nothing changed

Experts would keep reaching client calls on experience they had not confirmed and compliance steps they had skimmed in isolation. At this volume that is a standing business risk: weak data at the front door means worse matches and eligibility gaps downstream.

Desired outcome

One guided flow where the compliance-critical steps are reviewed together and the data that drives matching is captured in the same place, so experts are properly screened and current before they reach a client, and completion holds even with more to do.

The old flow: two flat tabs, no guided progression, and current experience sitting undifferentiated between personal details and the legal terms.

Research

Walking through the flow showed the problem was structure, not expert effort

The completion data showed me where experts were dropping off. Walking through the existing flow with my researcher, an informal expert review, showed me why. The issues clustered around structure: no clear information hierarchy, current and past experience blurred together, the most compliance-sensitive content given no more prominence than anything else, and key steps living outside the flow entirely.

These were structural problems, not visual ones, and they had the same effect: an expert could complete the flow without ever engaging with the data Dialectica depended on.

Design decisions

Four changes to get compliance-critical data reviewed, without hurting completion

Each change stands on its own and maps back to the problem. The broader structural move, two flat tabs rebuilt into a three-step sidebar with sub-steps, is the container they sit in.

Decision 1

Current experience got its own section, first in the flow

Before

Personal details, experience history and legal terms sat together on one crowded page. Current experience had no more visual weight than anything around it, so an expert could scroll straight past the one thing the business most needed confirmed. Past positions were not shown at all.

After

The Experiences sub-step now opens with current position at the top, in its own clearly labelled section. Past positions sit below in a compact list, a new addition. Personal details move to the bottom; the required ones must be confirmed before moving on.

Why

This is the headline compliance and data-accuracy move. The problem was never that experts refused to check their data; it was that the structure gave the most important data no prominence. Weight follows importance, and accurate profiles are what let Dialectica match an expert to the right project.

Decision 2

The Compliance Tutorial and Questionnaire moved inside the flow

Before

Both reached experts as separate emails, outside the main experience. They read as extra admin, homework to do on the side, each handled in isolation rather than as part of screening.

After

Both became dedicated sub-steps of Getting Started, in one guided sequence. The Tutorial presents compliance obligations to read and confirm; the Questionnaire asks client-configured questions where certain answers automatically disqualify an expert from that project. Each sub-step has one job and one confirm.

Why

Eligibility is the other half of what has to be true before a client call. Scattering the steps across emails made screening feel optional and easy to skim. Single-purpose sub-steps keep momentum even though there is more to do, which is what let the extra steps land without denting completion.

Each sub-step has one job and one confirm, with the sidebar holding the sequence together.

Decision 3

Availability slots became editable in place, not delete-and-redo

Before

The availability step was already a weekly calendar, but slots were fixed. Once created, a slot was permanent. To fix a time you deleted it and built a new one from scratch.

After

The calendar was redesigned so slots became editable. Experts place a slot on the grid, then keep control of it: drag to move it, or click to open a side menu and edit it fully. Correcting a slot is now a small adjustment.

Why

Availability is what lets Dialectica actually schedule the call the whole flow is building toward, so lowering the effort here has a direct operational payoff. It is the one change with a clean, defensible number attached, because the design is the direct mechanism.

Comparing the same months a year apart, the share of experts submitting availability rose about 4 percentage points, from about 70% to about 74%.

Decision 4

Experts pick categories and vendors from a shortlist instead of typing them

Before

The product-category and vendor data was chased separately over email, outside Terms of Engagement, after experts had already moved on. It depended on experts coming back to a message, so few ever gave it.

After

It became "Profile Boost," a skippable step inside onboarding. The shipped v2 splits capture into two lighter steps: pick from a shortlist of product categories (or add your own), then pick the vendors behind them (or add your own).

Why

Matching quality depends on structured, expert-provided detail. Capturing it inside the flow, while experts are already engaged, gets richer data than an email chase. Selecting is far quicker than typing from a blank field, and it showed.

Step 1 picks the product categories, step 2 the vendors behind each one. Conversion into the step roughly doubled, from about 17% to about 33%, with richer data too: about 6.5 vendors and 3.4 categories per expert, up from 4.2 and 2.0.

Experiment

Moving Terms to the end lifted the metric we were chasing and hurt the one that pays

Once the redesign had held its completion rate for two weeks, we tested a reordering. Terms sat early inside Getting Started, and our hypothesis was that experts sign it and then leave, because signing is the one thing they came to do. Move Terms behind Profile Boost, the reasoning went, and experts would pass through every step to reach it, giving us far more expertise data.

We ran it at 25% of experts for two weeks. The data side worked; the flow side did not. Making experts walk the full length of the funnel before the thing they came for made it feel long, and they abandoned it, some writing in to say so. An expert who skips Profile Boost still takes calls; an expert who never signs Terms takes none. That was the wrong trade, so we kept the shipped order.

The shipped order kept Terms early; the tested order moved it behind Profile Boost.

+20% Profile Boost completion in the test
-10% Terms signing, the step the business earns from

Outcome

Two new mandatory steps added, and completion held flat at about 90%

Adding the Tutorial and Questionnaire into the flow meant more mandatory steps than before. The textbook consequence of added friction is drop-off. It did not happen. The flat line is the result: it says the structure absorbed the extra complexity because each new step read as a natural part of one guided journey rather than another task bolted on.

~90% Completion, flat before and after the redesign
+4 pts Experts submitting availability, about 70% to 74%
~2x Profile Boost conversion, about 17% to 33%

Earlier exploration

Inline experience editing, designed then cut

Experts were first meant to correct their experience directly in the flow: edit pencils on each role and a single "confirm all experiences are up to date" checkbox. That version needed a whole review layer behind it, a dashboard where Dialectica associates could vet each submitted change before it went live. The overhead was not justified at this volume, so the shipped design used a lighter "Request a change" path that flags inaccurate data for manual review.

Showing the cut concept against the shipped pattern is deliberate: the idea was explored properly, and the scope call was a decision rather than a limitation nobody noticed.

The cut inline-edit concept. The shipped flow uses a lighter "Request a change" path instead.

Profile Boost from v1 to v2

A ready list of categories and vendors meant experts never started from zero, and conversion roughly doubled

v1 was the first in-flow version: a skippable step where experts mapped the products they can speak to against the vendors behind them, typed as free text. A parallel layout exploration tried putting Profile Boost on the same step as personal details and experience, surfaced through a per-company "Boost your profile" modal, before it settled as its own dedicated step. v2 then redesigned the capture into the two shortlisted steps that shipped, which roughly doubled uptake.
